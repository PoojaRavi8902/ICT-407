2.1 — Input Devices and Their Uses

Definition: An input device is any hardware used to enter data or instructions into a computer system for processing.

Complete device list (syllabus coverage)

Keyboard, numeric keypad, pointing devices, remote control, joystick/driving wheel, touch screen, scanners, camera, microphone, sensors, light pen.

Detailed notes — device by device

Keyboard
Description: Standard text entry device — letters, numbers, function & control keys.
Uses: Word processing, programming, data entry, shortcuts in exams (calculations & commands).
Advantages: Easy for text, accurate after practice, inexpensive and widely available.
Disadvantages: Slow for mass numeric entry, takes desk space, risk of RSI.
Exam tip: If question asks about numeric data entry, mention the numeric keypad separately and compare speed/accuracy.
Numeric keypad
Description: Calculator style block for fast numeric entry.
Uses: ATMs, POS terminals, calculators, phone number entry.
Advantages: Efficient for numbers, compact. Disadvantages: Not suitable for text; limited in functionality.
Pointing devices (mouse, touch‑pad, trackball)
Uses: GUI navigation, drawing, selection.
Advantages: Intuitive; accurate for graphics and selection.
Disadvantages: Mouse needs flat surface; touch‑pad less precise for drawing; not ideal for phones.
Remote control
Description & Uses: Wireless hand‑held transmitter for TVs, presentations, drones.
Advantages: Operates at a distance; increases convenience.
Disadvantages: Range limitations, line‑of‑sight (IR), batteries required.
Joystick / Driving wheel
Uses: Gaming, simulators, robotics control.
Advantages: Realistic directional control; precision for simulation tasks.
Disadvantages: Specialist; mechanical wear & maintenance.
Touch screen (input)
Types: Capacitive, resistive, infrared.
Uses: ATMs, kiosks, smartphones, ticket machines.
Advantages: Direct interaction; combines input & output; intuitive.
Disadvantages: Fingerprints, reduced visibility, less precision for detailed tasks, cleaning/calibration required.
Scanner
Types: Flat‑bed, hand‑held, document feeder, barcode/QR scanners.
Uses: Digitising documents, photographs, reading barcodes & QR codes.
Advantages: Accurate digital copies; quick for printed material.
Disadvantages: Large file sizes at high resolution; quality depends on resolution and lighting.
Camera
Uses: Video conferencing, security, facial recognition, image capture for documents.
Advantages: Portable, produces digital evidence/visuals.
Disadvantages: Dependent on lighting & resolution; privacy concerns.
Microphone
Uses: Voice recognition, audio recording, telephony & conferencing.
Advantages: Enables hands‑free input and accessibility features.
Disadvantages: Picks up background noise; quality varies by hardware.
Sensors
Examples & Uses: Temperature (climate control), light (auto‑lighting), motion (security), pressure (industrial measurement).
Advantages: Enables automation and continuous monitoring; useful in hazardous/remote areas.
Disadvantages: Requires calibration; can fail in extreme environments; potential maintenance overhead.
Light pen
Description & Uses: Stylus that detects CRT screen light for selection and drawing (historic but syllabus mentions it).
Advantages: Precise selection for CAD tasks.
Disadvantages: Works only with CRTs; can be tiring to use.
Exam tip: In 6‑mark or 8‑mark questions, structure your answer: Identify → Justify suitability → State one advantage → State one disadvantage. Use a short scenario sentence: "For ____, use ____ because..."

2.2 — Direct Data Entry & Associated Devices

Definition: Direct data entry devices input data automatically from a source (no manual typing required).

Devices and notes

Magnetic stripe reader
Uses: Credit/debit cards, ID cards, hotel keys.
Advantages: Fast and simple.
Disadvantages: Low storage; stripe wear or damage.
Chip & PIN reader
Uses: Secure card payments.
Advantages: Encrypted security, prevents cloning.
Disadvantages: Requires PIN; damaged chip disables card.
RFID reader
Uses: Contactless payments, tracking, passports.
Advantages: Reads multiple tags; no line of sight.
Disadvantages: High setup cost; security/privacy risks.
OMR (Optical Mark Recognition)
Uses: Multiple‑choice tests, surveys.
Advantages: Fast & accurate for mass marking.
Disadvantages: Strict form design; damaged marks cause errors.
OCR (Optical Character Recognition)
Uses: Convert printed pages to editable text.
Advantages: Fast conversion, saves typing time.
Disadvantages: Struggles with handwriting, requires proofreading.
Bar code reader
Uses: Retail, stock management, hospital bands.
Advantages: Quick & accurate.
Disadvantages: Needs line of sight; damaged codes fail.
QR scanner
Uses: Links to websites, tickets, payments.
Advantages: Stores more data; omnidirectional read.
Disadvantages: Requires camera; poor lighting reduces reliability.
Scenario practice: "A supermarket checkout needs the fastest method to scan thousands of items per day." → Recommend a barcode reader (or multiple scanners) — justify: speed, accuracy, low per‑item cost; mention disadvantage: damaged labels and line‑of‑sight.
Exam tip: For OMR/OCR questions, mention form/template design and a short note about validation or proof‑reading to gain extra marks.

2.3 — Output Devices and Their Uses

Definition: An output device displays or presents processed data to users in visual, auditory, or physical form.

Device notes

Monitor / Screen
Types: CRT (historic), LCD, LED.
Uses: Displaying interfaces, documents, photos, video.
Advantages: Immediate visual feedback, good colour and resolution (modern screens).
Disadvantages: Power consumption and portability issues (especially older types).
Touch screen (output)
Uses: ATMs, ticket machines, phones.
Advantages: Intuitive, combines output with direct input.
Disadvantages: Smudges, glare and limited precision.
Multimedia projector
Uses: Classrooms, meetings, public display.
Advantages: Large shared display; good for audiences.
Disadvantages: Needs darkened room; lamp replacement costs.
Printers
Laser: High‑volume text, fast and quiet; costly toner.
Inkjet: Good for photos and small office use; slower and ink may smudge.
Dot‑matrix: For continuous forms and harsh environments; noisy and low quality.
3D printer: Creates physical objects from models; slow and material costs are high.
Plotter
Uses: Engineering drawings, CAD prints.
Advantages: High precision, large format.
Disadvantages: Slow and expensive; niche use.
Speakers
Uses: Audio playback for media, alarms, notifications.
Advantages: Sound feedback and accessibility for visually impaired.
Disadvantages: Noise pollution; unsuited to quiet environments.
Actuator
Description & Uses: Converts electrical signals into physical movement (robot arms, automated doors).
Advantages: Enables physical automation and control systems.
Disadvantages: Costly, requires safety control and maintenance.
Exam checklist — output devices
  1. Always name the device and purpose.
  2. State one clear advantage and one clear limitation.
  3. Link the device to the scenario (audience, environment, cost).
Practical example

School open day: choose projector for large audience + handouts printed by inkjet/laser. Mention dark room & cost trade‑offs.

Mastery & Exam Practice

Below are high‑yield question types and model answer structure — ideal for classroom tests and revision.

Model question (6 marks)

Question: "A small clinic needs to record patient arrival times and print name labels. Recommend one input device and one output device. Justify your choices and state one advantage and one disadvantage for each."

Model answer structure:

  1. Input device: Barcode/QR scanner — Justification: quick patient check‑in, reduces typing errors. Advantage: fast; Disadvantage: barcode labels may be damaged.
  2. Output device: Thermal/inkjet label printer — Justification: prints durable name labels at point of care. Advantage: quick/portable; Disadvantage: consumable costs (ink/labels).
Model question (8 marks)

Question: "Compare OCR and OMR for use in marking exam papers. In your answer discuss speed, accuracy, cost and suitability."

Answer pointers:

  • Definition of OCR vs OMR.
  • Speed — OMR is faster for MCQs; OCR suitable for printed long answers but requires proofreading.
  • Accuracy — OMR very accurate for filled bubbles; OCR error prone with poor print/handwriting.
  • Cost & setup — OMR requires standardized forms; OCR needs software and proofreading staff.
  • Suitability — OMR for MCQs; OCR for legacy printed documents needing digital conversion.
Revision tip: Make a short two‑column A4 cheat sheet: left = device; right = 1 use + 1 advantage + 1 disadvantage. Use that for quick recall.

  1. Always link device choice to the scenario.

  3. Write concise bullets — examiners reward clarity.
  4. If unsure, pick a conservative, low‑cost option and justify with cost & simplicity.
