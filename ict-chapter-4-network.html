4.1 Networks

4.1.1 Introduction to Networks

A network is a group of two or more computers or devices connected together so they can share resources, exchange data, and communicate with each other.

Networks can be as small as two computers in a room or as large as the internet, which connects millions of devices worldwide.

Resources that can be shared include:

  • Files
  • Printers and scanners
  • Internet connections
  • Storage space

Why networks are important in ICT

  • Enable collaboration (e.g., students working together on shared documents).
  • Facilitate communication (e.g., email, messaging, video calls).
  • Reduce cost (e.g., sharing printers rather than buying one for each computer).

4.1.2 Storage Devices in Networks

Magnetic Storage

Characteristics: Uses magnetic surfaces to read/write data.

Examples:

  • Fixed magnetic hard drives (HDD) – inside desktop computers.
  • Portable hard drives – external backup drives.
  • Magnetic tape drives – used for large-scale backups in businesses.

Advantages:

  • High capacity.
  • Cheap per gigabyte.
  • Reliable for long-term storage.

Disadvantages:

  • Slower than solid-state.
  • Fragile (moving parts can fail).
  • Not very portable compared to flash drives.

Optical Storage

Characteristics: Data stored by burning pits onto a disc’s surface.

Examples:

  • CD (700 MB)
  • DVD (4.7 GB)
  • Blu-ray (25 GB or more)

Advantages:

  • Cheap and portable.
  • Good for distribution (e.g., movies, software).

Disadvantages:

  • Easily scratched.
  • Low storage capacity compared to other media.
  • Slower read/write speeds.

Solid-State Storage

Characteristics: Uses flash memory chips. No moving parts.

Examples: Solid State Drives (SSD), Pen drives (USB sticks), Flash memory cards (SD, microSD, CompactFlash, CFast).

Advantages:

  • Fast read/write speeds.
  • Very portable and durable.
  • Lower power consumption.

Disadvantages:

  • More expensive than magnetic storage.
  • Limited number of write cycles.

4.1.3 Common Network Devices

Router

Purpose: Connects different networks together (usually your local home/office network to the internet).

Functions:

  • Routes data packets between networks.
  • Stores device addresses (IP addresses).
  • Provides firewall/security features.

Real-life example: Your Wi-Fi router at home lets phones, laptops, and smart TVs access the internet.

Other Devices

  • Network Interface Card (NIC): Hardware inside a device that allows it to connect to a network (wired or wireless).
  • Hub: A simple device that broadcasts data to all connected devices (rarely used today).
  • Switch: Smarter than a hub, sends data only to the intended device. Improves speed and reduces collisions.
  • Bridge: Connects two different networks and filters traffic.

4.1.4 Wireless Technologies

Wi-Fi

Characteristics: Wireless technology that uses radio signals to connect devices to a network.

Uses: Connecting laptops, tablets, and smartphones to the internet.

Advantages:

  • No need for cables.
  • Can connect many devices at once.

Disadvantages:

  • Signal strength can be weak in large buildings.
  • Less secure than wired connections if not encrypted.

Bluetooth

Characteristics: Short-range wireless communication (usually 10 meters).

Uses:

  • Connecting headphones, keyboards, mice.
  • Transferring files between phones.

Advantages:

  • Low power usage.
  • Easy to connect (pairing).

Disadvantages:

  • Very short range.
  • Slower speeds than Wi-Fi.

4.1.5 Cloud Computing

Definition: Storing, managing, and processing data on remote servers accessed via the internet instead of using a local computer or storage device.

Examples: Google Drive, OneDrive, Dropbox, iCloud.

Advantages

  • Access data from anywhere with internet.
  • Share files easily.
  • No need to buy expensive hardware for storage.
  • Automatic backups provided.

Disadvantages

  • Needs internet connection.
  • Privacy/security concerns (data stored on third-party servers).
  • Ongoing cost (subscriptions).

4.1.6 Common Network Environments

Intranet: Private network within an organization (e.g., school or company).

Extranet: Controlled private network allowing external partners (e.g., suppliers) limited access.

Internet: Global system of connected networks.

4.1.7 Network Types

  • LAN (Local Area Network): Covers a small area (e.g., school, office). High speed, privately owned.
  • WLAN (Wireless LAN): LAN that uses Wi-Fi. Convenient but less secure.
  • WAN (Wide Area Network): Covers large areas, often connecting multiple LANs. Internet is the largest WAN.

4.2 Network Issues and Communication

4.2.1 Security Issues

Passwords

Prevent unauthorized access.

Strong password features: at least 8–12 characters, mix of upper/lower case, numbers, symbols.

Weak password examples: “123456”, “password”, “abc123”.

Authentication Methods

  • Zero login (single sign-on systems).
  • Biometric methods: fingerprints, face recognition, retina scan.
  • Magnetic stripes and smart cards.
  • Physical/electronic tokens (e.g., RSA token that generates one-time passwords).

Anti-malware Software

Detects, removes, and quarantines viruses.

  • Must be updated regularly.
  • Used to scan: storage media (USB, hard drives) and downloaded files/software.

4.2.2 Privacy and Confidentiality

Sensitive data must be protected from interception.

Methods

  • Use encryption.
  • Regularly update anti-spyware.
  • Change passwords often.

4.2.3 Electronic Conferencing

Types

Video Conferencing: Requires webcam, microphone, internet, and conferencing software (Zoom, Teams).

Advantages: face-to-face communication across distance, saves travel cost.

Disadvantages: needs strong internet, time zone issues.

Audio Conferencing: Voice-only meetings using telephones or internet VoIP. Cheaper than video conferencing.

Web Conferencing: Shared online platform where participants can collaborate on documents, chat, and share screens. Used for online teaching, webinars, business meetings.
